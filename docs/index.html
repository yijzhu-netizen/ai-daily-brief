Project Freedom: Strait of Hormuz Crisis — A Data-Driven Assessment

Published: May 4, 2026

On May 4, 2026, the Trump administration launched Project Freedom, a military operation to reopen the Strait of Hormuz — the world's most critical oil chokepoint — after Iran's closure brought global shipping to a halt. Here is the data-driven breakdown of what is happening and what it means.

The Military Footprint: By the Numbers

U.S. Central Command (CENTCOM) deployed a layered defensive "umbrella" across the strait. Admiral Brad Cooper confirmed the following force structure: 15,000 service members, over 100 land- and sea-based aircraft, ballistic missile defense-capable destroyers (with at least 12 destroyers positioned in the Middle East as of late April), and multi-domain unmanned platforms operating under, on, and above the sea surface. Within hours of launch, two U.S. Navy destroyers transited the strait under direct Iranian fire — facing a barrage of small boats, cruise missiles, and drones. CENTCOM reported destroying 6 Iranian small boats in the initial engagement.

Global Shipping: 87 Countries Stranded

The strait's closure stranded vessels from 87 countries in the Persian Gulf. CENTCOM stated it redirected at least 49 ships and contacted "dozens of shipping companies" to encourage resumed traffic. Yet shipping executives remain unconvinced. As one analyst at CNBC noted, the core problem is uncertainty about transit safety — ship captains and insurers are hesitant to take the risk even with a military escort in place.

Oil Markets Under Pressure

The International Energy Agency (IEA) described the disruption as "the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market." Global oil supply crashed by 10.1 million barrels per day (mb/d) in March 2026 alone due to attacks on energy infrastructure and tanker restrictions. ICE Brent crude is now projected at $104/bbl in Q2 2026, falling to $92/bbl in Q4 2026 on the assumption of a gradual reopening. Current spot prices oscillated between $90 and $100 during the late April ceasefire window.

Beyond Oil: The Hidden Commodity Impact

The crisis extends beyond crude. Approximately one-third of global seaborne methanol trade passes through the Strait of Hormuz, threatening chemical feedstocks for resins, coatings, and plastics. The World Economic Forum flagged 9 non-oil commodities — including aluminum, sulfur, and graphite — that face supply chain pressure from the blockage, with direct consequences for global manufacturing and the green energy transition.

What's Next: Ceasefire at a Crossroads

Trump initially announced then paused Project Freedom within 48 hours, signaling a willingness to negotiate. But Iran's military has vowed to attack any U.S. forces entering the strait, and the fragile ceasefire faces its biggest test. The outcome will determine whether the $104/bbl oil price trajectory holds — or accelerates higher.

— Data compiled from CENTCOM briefings, IEA reports, CNBC, CNN, and WEF analysis on May 4, 2026.
